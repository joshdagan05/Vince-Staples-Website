VINCE STAPLES

ABOUT

Vince Staples, born Vincent Jamal Staples on July 2, 1993, in Compton, California, grew up in a turbulent environment shaped by gang culture, violence, and socioeconomic challenges. He spent his formative years in Long Beach, California, specifically in the Ramona Park neighborhood, which would later influence much of his music. Raised in a large family, Staples faced the pressures of gang life early on, as many of his peers and relatives were involved in local gangs.

While his surroundings often drew people into cycles of crime, Vince initially managed to stay out of trouble, thanks in part to his mother, who sought to keep him away from dangerous influences. However, as a teenager, Staples became more deeply involved in the street life that surrounded him, eventually affiliating with the Crips gang. His experiences growing up in these challenging circumstances, witnessing violence, and the struggles of the people around him would later serve as the foundation for much of his storytelling in music.

Vince Staples didn't initially see music as a viable career path. His introduction to the music scene came through friendships with members of the hip-hop collective Odd Future, particularly Earl Sweatshirt. Staples would accompany Earl and other members of the group to the studio, where he began to see the potential of using music as an outlet for his thoughts and experiences. This exposure to the recording process and the encouragement of his peers led him to consider rap as a serious pursuit.

His breakthrough came in 2011 when he appeared on the track "epaR" from Earl Sweatshirt's mixtape *Earl*. The track gave him his first taste of recognition within the underground rap scene. Encouraged by the positive reception, Staples began working on his own music. His first solo project, a mixtape titled *Shyne Coldchain Vol. 1*, was released in 2011, showcasing his raw storytelling and gritty lyrics that reflected the harsh realities of his upbringing.

Staples gained further momentum when he caught the attention of veteran rapper and producer Mac Miller, who invited him to collaborate. Together, they worked on Vinceâ€™s *Stolen Youth* mixtape in 2013, with Miller producing much of the project under the alias Larry Fisherman. The mixtape helped Staples garner critical acclaim and introduced him to a wider audience. His lyrics stood out for their sharp, observational style and introspective nature, focusing on themes such as poverty, violence, and existential struggles.

In 2014, Staples signed to Def Jam Recordings and released his debut EP, *Hell Can Wait*, which included the popular single "Hands Up," a politically charged track addressing police brutality. His debut studio album, *Summertime '06*, followed in 2015, marking a turning point in his career. The album, produced largely by No I.D., was a critical success, praised for its dense production and candid exploration of his life growing up in Long Beach.

Vince Staples has continued to evolve as an artist, balancing mainstream success with his commitment to telling authentic, unvarnished stories about his community. His music is a reflection of the duality of his experiences: navigating between the violence of his youth and his efforts to rise above it.
